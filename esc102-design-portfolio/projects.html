Project 01

Praxis I: Backpack Strap Design

ESC101 Praxis I | Fall 2025 | Team project

Stakeholders

Students carrying heavy backpacks, teammates, prototype evaluators

Main challenge

Reduce excess-strap snagging without losing adjustability or comfort

Outcome

Three concept directions compared with a simplified ergonomic simulation and explicit performance metrics

Summary

The project started as a redesign problem around excess backpack straps catching on external objects. The more useful framing was broader: the loose strap was only one symptom of a system that also involved comfort, access to the bag, adjustability, and manufacturability. The team explored three concept directions, and I supported that comparison by building a simplified lumbar-deviation model that converted prototype parameters into legible metrics.

The value of the project was not in claiming an exact human-body model. It was in creating a structured way to compare directions that otherwise would have remained qualitative. Once the metrics were explicit, the conversation could move from "which idea seems cleaner" to "which idea changes load distance, pressure, or obstruction in a defensible way."

Evidence to open first

  • Evidence sheet PDF for concept notes, criteria, and simulation captures.
  • MATLAB source for the comparison model and parameter choices.

Process Notes

What changed during the design process

The project improved once the strap problem was framed more widely and the representation was made answer to clearer criteria.

Frame Shift

The problem widened before the model mattered

My first instinct was to treat the strap issue as a visible nuisance that needed a clever fastening mechanism. The project got stronger once the team treated it as a system of consequences involving snagging, posture, load path, ease of adjustment, and fabrication.

Representation Shift

The concepts stayed separate long enough to compare

Divergence was useful because the concepts were kept distinct instead of collapsing into a hybrid too early. My contribution was to represent those directions through parameters such as excess strap, load distance, pack weight, and pressure, so the model could support comparison rather than replace it.

What I Learned

Criteria had to exist before the simulation could help

Modeling only became useful after the team had made success legible. Before that, the simulation would have produced numbers without a decision. After the criteria were visible, even a simplified model could expose where a concept shifted the burden somewhere else.

My role

Team contribution and credit

  • Concept generation and design direction were shared team work.
  • I built the simulation-based comparison tool and helped define the measurable criteria used to compare concepts.
  • The final interpretation of prototype directions and requirements was collaborative, not individual.

Next time I would pair the early simulation with faster physical checks rather than letting representation carry the whole burden.

Project 02

CIV102 Bridge

CIV102 | Fall 2025 | Team 404

Stakeholders

Bridge team members, course load test, fabrication constraints

Main challenge

Design a matboard bridge that survives the moving train load within geometry and material limits

Outcome

Engineering drawings, hand calculations, and analysis code for load envelopes, section properties, and failure modes

Summary

The CIV102 bridge required a design that could survive a specified moving train load under tight material and fabrication constraints. The analytical work centered on finding the critical bending and shear regions, computing section properties, and mapping likely failure modes such as tension, compression, web buckling, shear, and glue failure.

What makes the project important in this portfolio is not that it contains many calculations. It is that the calculations had to be tied to buildable geometry. The final bridge could not be treated as an abstract optimum. It had to be drawn, cut, glued, and assembled with the same logic that the analysis assumed.

Evidence to open first

  • Design report PDF for the full design rationale and summary.
  • Engineering drawings for geometry, spacing, and assembly logic.
  • Calculation package for failure checks and factor-of-safety work.
  • Python analysis for moving-load envelopes and section comparisons.

Process Notes

Why the analytical frame had to widen

The bridge only became convincing once the calculations started answering to failure modes, geometry, and build logic at the same time.

Early Trap

Numerical neatness looked more complete than it was

Early on, the bridge invited a familiar habit in my own process: treating the design as a problem of numerical optimization around material efficiency. That framing was attractive because it produced clean variables and crisp output, but it was incomplete.

Useful Shift

Failure modes made the analysis genuinely helpful

The work improved once the bridge was represented as interacting failure modes under a moving load rather than a single strength number. That shift made the envelope analysis, section properties, and buckling checks useful because they were tied to specific regions and construction decisions.

Hard Lesson

Constructability needed equal weight before convergence

The project made visible a design lesson that pure analysis can hide: drawings, glue strategy, fit tolerances, and assembly quality had to be represented early enough to change the geometry, not just explain it afterward.

My role

Team contribution and credit

  • Bridge geometry selection, report production, and fabrication were shared team work.
  • I contributed analytical modeling, section-property work, load-envelope reasoning, and failure-mode interpretation through code and calculations.
  • The drawings and final bridge were collective outputs and should be read that way.

Project 03

Gaussian Entanglement Project

Independent research | Winter 2026 | Individual

Stakeholders

Myself as researcher, future collaborators, technical readers

Main challenge

Derive and test closed-form entanglement thresholds for symmetric Gaussian channels

Outcome

Analytic threshold expressions, comparison figures, and parameter- space checks across multiple channel regimes

Summary

This project studies when entanglement survives after a two-mode squeezed vacuum state passes through symmetric phase-insensitive Gaussian channels. The central design move was to change the representation of the problem so the partially transposed eigenvalue condition could be treated as an explicit inequality in the channel parameters.

In portfolio terms, this is not a conventional product-design artifact. It is a method design problem. The challenge was to decide what structure to preserve, how to state the analytical claim clearly, and how to use figures and exact comparisons to revise the cleaner formulation before trusting it. That makes it a useful counterpoint to the more physical design projects in the portfolio.

Evidence to open first

  • Long note PDF for the derivation and framing of the problem.
  • Comparison script for exact-versus- bound checks, regime scans, and figures.

Process Notes

Why abstraction was the main design decision

The work only gained leverage once the analytical claim was explicit, the supporting argument was inspectable, and the figures were used to revise the claim rather than decorate it.

Analytical Claim

Symmetry turned the condition into a direct analytical statement

The first version of the problem could have remained a repeated numerical evaluation of the full symplectic spectrum. The better move was to ask whether symmetry could reduce the condition to something invertible and interpretable at the parameter level.

PIAA Loop

Plots only mattered when they changed what I claimed next

After the cleaner condition was in place, the figures became decision tools. I used them to perceive where the bound separated from exact behavior, interpret what feature caused the gap, assess whether the shortcut was still acceptable, and act by revising the way I described the result.

Argument Discipline

The claim only held if the grounds and warrant stayed visible

The main tradeoff was generality versus tractability. The cleaner statement only earned its authority when I could show the claim itself, the mathematical grounds supporting it, and the warrant that explains why the reduced setting was still informative. That discipline kept the exclusions visible.

My role

Contribution and credit

  • This is an individual research project.
  • I carried out the analytic framing, coding, parameter sweeps, figure generation, and draft write-up.
  • Any future extension toward publication would require a clearer collaboration structure, but the work presented here is mine.
