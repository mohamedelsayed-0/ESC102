Project 01

Praxis I: Backpack Strap Design

ESC101 Praxis I | Fall 2025 | Team project

Stakeholders

Students carrying heavy backpacks, teammates, prototype evaluators

Main challenge

Reduce excess-strap snagging without losing adjustability or comfort

Outcome

Three concept directions compared with a simplified ergonomic simulation and explicit performance metrics

Summary

The project started as a redesign problem around excess backpack straps catching on external objects. The more useful framing was broader: the loose strap was only one symptom of a system that also involved comfort, access to the bag, adjustability, and manufacturability. The team explored three concept directions, and I supported that comparison by building a simplified lumbar-deviation model that converted prototype parameters into legible metrics.

The value of the project was not in claiming an exact human-body model. It was in creating a structured way to compare directions that otherwise would have remained qualitative. Once the metrics were explicit, the conversation could move from "which idea seems cleaner" to "which idea changes load distance, pressure, or obstruction in a defensible way."

Evidence to open first

  • Evidence sheet PDF for concept notes, criteria, and simulation captures.
  • MATLAB source for the comparison model and parameter choices.

Process Notes

What changed during the design process

The project improved once the strap problem was widened into a carrying-system problem and the comparison model was made answer to explicit criteria. This is the first case where my preference for structure helps the team, but also shows its limits.

Reframe

The loose strap was only one visible symptom of a larger carrying problem

My first instinct was to hunt for a neat fastening mechanism. The project got stronger once the team treated the issue as a system of consequences involving snagging, posture, load path, ease of adjustment, and manufacturability. That wider frame is what justified keeping three concept directions open long enough to compare them meaningfully instead of converging on the cleanest-looking fix.

Design Lesson

The simulation helped because it disciplined comparison, not because it was exact

My contribution was to translate each concept into comparable parameters such as excess strap, load distance, and pressure, then tie those outputs back to explicit criteria. That made the model useful, but it also exposed a recurring risk in my own process: when build evidence is thin, representation can start looking more certain than the assumptions behind it. The project would have been stronger still if the early model had been paired with faster physical checks.

My role

My contribution

  • I built the simulation-based comparison tool and helped define the measurable criteria used to compare concepts.
  • I used the model outputs to support comparison between concept directions instead of treating the simulation as a final answer on its own.

Team credit

  • Concept generation and design direction were shared team work.
  • The final interpretation of prototype directions and requirements was collaborative, not individual.

Project 02

CIV102 Bridge

CIV102 | Fall 2025 | Team 404

Stakeholders

Bridge team members, course load test, fabrication constraints

Main challenge

Design a matboard bridge that survives the moving train load within geometry and material limits

Outcome

Engineering drawings, hand calculations, and analysis code for load envelopes, section properties, and failure modes

Summary

The CIV102 bridge required a design that could survive a specified moving train load under tight material and fabrication constraints. The analytical work centered on finding the critical bending and shear regions, computing section properties, and mapping likely failure modes such as tension, compression, web buckling, shear, and glue failure.

What makes the project important in this portfolio is not that it contains many calculations. It is that the calculations had to be tied to buildable geometry. The final bridge could not be treated as an abstract optimum. It had to be drawn, cut, glued, and assembled with the same logic that the analysis assumed.

Evidence to open first

  • Design report PDF for the full design rationale and summary.
  • Engineering drawings for geometry, spacing, and assembly logic.
  • Calculation package for failure checks and factor-of-safety work.
  • Python analysis for moving-load envelopes and section comparisons.

Process Notes

Why the analytical frame had to widen

The bridge only became convincing once the calculations started answering to failure modes, geometry, and build logic at the same time. This is where my attraction to clean quantitative structure was most obviously incomplete.

Reframe

The bridge stopped being a neat optimization problem once failure modes and build logic entered

Early on, the project invited a familiar habit: treating the bridge as a problem of material efficiency with a clean numerical objective. The work got stronger once the design was represented instead as interacting failure modes under a moving load, with drawings and assembly decisions counted as part of the same reasoning chain. That shift made the calculations useful because they were answering to specific regions, connections, and fabrication consequences.

Design Lesson

Constructability needed to influence the geometry before convergence, not after it

The hardest lesson here was that drawings, glue strategy, fit tolerances, and assembly quality could not stay downstream of the math. If those build conditions are only used to explain the bridge afterward, then the cleanest section on paper gets too much authority. This case made visible that verification had to include the way the bridge would actually be cut, glued, and assembled, not just the way it looked inside the analytical model.

My role

My contribution

  • I contributed analytical modeling, section-property work, load-envelope reasoning, and failure-mode interpretation through code and calculations.
  • I helped connect the analytical checks to the regions and failure modes that mattered for the final bridge logic.

Team credit

  • Bridge geometry selection, report production, and fabrication were shared team work.
  • The drawings and final bridge were collective outputs and should be read that way.

Project 03

Praxis II: Daycare Wristband Workflow

ESC102 Praxis II | Winter 2026 | Team project

Stakeholders

Daycare staff, parents, children, classroom check-in lines

Main challenge

Reduce wristband workload and lateness risk without shifting hidden burden back onto staff or families

Outcome

Requirement matrix, beta simulation, and threshold visualizations for parent-applied and machine-assisted concepts

Summary

Praxis II centered on a daycare field-trip wristband process. The visible artifact was the wristband, but the real design problem was a morning workflow: one staff member had to manage attendance, supervision, forms, and wristband application under departure pressure. The team compared a parent-applied wristband concept against one- and two-machine dispensing concepts. My main contribution was the beta simulation and the metric logic that turned those directions into comparable operational cases.

The simulation mattered because it separated staff-time savings from end-to-end timing risk. Under the default beta assumptions, the current process used about 34.5 active staff minutes. A parent-applied flow dropped that to about 28.4 minutes and missed actual departure in about 17% of simulated days, while two machine stations dropped active staff time to about 20.3 minutes but still kept queue-driven risk visible. That difference stopped the project from calling the fastest-looking option "better" without saying under what conditions it stayed useful.

Evidence to open first

  • Beta release PDF for the requirement table, metric choices, and justification trail.
  • Interactive beta simulation for the browser model and adjustable assumptions.
  • Static report for the default-case charts and summary cards.
  • Simulation core for the threshold and risk logic used in the beta comparison.

Process Notes

Why the workflow frame mattered more than the device frame

The project got clearer once the opportunity was treated as morning workflow risk rather than a single product idea. That shift let the beta simulation compare where each concept actually moved work, timing pressure, and failure correction.

Reframe

The real problem was a crowded arrival routine, not only the wristband itself

The requirement table helped widen the project from "make wristbands easier" to "reduce operational burden while keeping safety, intuitiveness, and setup time acceptable." That mattered because a concept could look elegant as an object but still create a bad check-in process once parents, classroom verification, and fallback staff work were included.

Design Lesson

The beta simulation only helped because it split staff savings from timing risk

The useful move was not just running scenarios. It was tracking active staff time, completion risk, and break-even thresholds separately. That exposed results the team could actually act on: the parent concept became staff-time beneficial around 47% compliance at the default incorrect-application rate, one machine saved staff minutes but still missed departure too often, and two machines kept stronger staff savings while still forcing us to account for queue effects.

My role

My contribution

  • I built the beta simulation, defined the operational metrics used in the comparison, and generated the threshold charts and summary visuals.
  • I used the model to separate staff-time savings from timing risk so the team could compare concepts under stated conditions rather than from intuition alone.

Team credit

  • Stakeholder framing, concept generation, and the larger design rationale were shared team work.
  • The beta release argument should be read as a collaborative design effort supported by my modeling and evidence interpretation.

Project 04

Gaussian Entanglement Project

Independent research | Winter 2026 | Individual

Stakeholders

Myself as researcher, future collaborators, technical readers

Main challenge

Derive and test closed-form entanglement thresholds for symmetric Gaussian channels

Outcome

Analytic threshold expressions, comparison figures, and parameter- space checks across multiple channel regimes

Summary

This project studies when entanglement survives after a two-mode squeezed vacuum state passes through symmetric phase-insensitive Gaussian channels. The central design move was to change the representation of the problem so the partially transposed eigenvalue condition could be treated as an explicit inequality in the channel parameters.

In portfolio terms, this is not a conventional product-design artifact. It is a method design problem. The challenge was to decide what structure to preserve, how to state the analytical claim clearly, and how to use figures and exact comparisons to revise the cleaner formulation before trusting it. That makes it a useful counterpoint to the more physical design projects in the portfolio.

Evidence to open first

  • Long note PDF for the derivation and framing of the problem.
  • Comparison script for exact-versus- bound checks, regime scans, and figures.

Process Notes

Why abstraction was the main design decision

The work only gained leverage once the analytical claim was explicit, the supporting argument was inspectable, and the figures were used to revise the claim rather than decorate it. This case keeps the same structure as the earlier projects, but the design pressure is mostly on reasoning rather than fabrication.

Abstraction Choice

The useful move was turning repeated computation into a readable analytical claim

The first version of the problem could have remained a numerical routine around the full symplectic spectrum. The stronger move was to ask whether symmetry could reduce the condition to something explicit in the channel parameters. That shift mattered for the same reason the earlier project reframings mattered: it changed what kind of decision the evidence could support.

Design Lesson

The cleaner threshold only earned authority when the figures changed the argument

After the reduced condition was in place, the plots became decision tools rather than decorations. I used them to perceive where the bound separated from exact behavior, interpret what caused the gap, assess whether the shortcut was still acceptable, and act by tightening the claim. The real discipline here was keeping the claim, grounds, and warrant visible together so tractability did not get mistaken for completeness.

My role

My contribution

  • I carried out the analytic framing, coding, parameter sweeps, figure generation, and draft write-up.
  • Any future extension toward publication would require a clearer collaboration structure, but the work presented here is mine.

Credit note

  • This is an individual research project.
