Position in Context

How I currently approach engineering design

My strongest design habit is turning a vague problem into a structure that can be tested. My biggest recurring risk is trusting that structure too quickly once it becomes mathematically clean.

Design priorities
Traceability, explicit tradeoffs, and calibrated simplification
Known bias
Converging too early when a quantitative objective becomes available
Growth direction
Represent constructability and communication earlier, not after the model is built

Position Statement

Formal statement

I approach engineering design by first trying to make the situation legible. In practice, that means identifying which stakeholders matter, which constraints are actually active, and which variables can change a decision rather than merely decorate a report. I am most effective when a problem is still under-specified and needs a structure that turns it into something a team can compare, test, or communicate. Across the work in this portfolio, that structure takes different forms: a stakeholder-and-metric frame in Praxis I, a failure-mode and geometry model in CIV102, and an invariant reduction in the Gaussian project.

Under ambiguity, I tend to build a simplified representation early. I do not see that first model as the truth of the problem. I use it as a decision scaffold. In Praxis I, the point of the backpack simulation was not to claim exact biomechanics, but to force the team to compare prototype directions against explicit criteria such as deviation, pressure, added weight, and obstruction. In CIV102, the analytical model was useful because it decomposed the bridge into failure modes and load envelopes that could guide geometry choices. In the Gaussian work, the key move was to choose a representation that made the entanglement condition analytically invertible. In each case, the design gain came from selecting a productive representation, not from presenting a polished final result.

Evidence matters to me when it can actually move a decision. I prefer calculations, plots, comparisons, and drawings that reveal what should change next. That preference is a strength because it helps me avoid vague justification and gives teams a basis for discussion. It is also a weakness. Once I find a workable mathematical or computational handle, I can start to overweight what is easy to quantify. That can narrow the problem too quickly around the most tractable criterion rather than the most important one. The bridge project exposed this clearly: strong calculations did not compensate for the need to represent build quality, splice location, glue use, and diaphragm fit as design constraints in their own right.

My default balance between speed and rigor is to move quickly toward a coarse structure, then deepen rigor where failure risk or disagreement appears. I am comfortable with early simplification, but I now see more clearly that simplification only helps if I keep asking what it leaves out. In teams I often occupy the role of model-builder or analytical translator. That role is valuable when it turns rough ideas into shared criteria, but it can also hide team dependencies if I fail to represent communication and fabrication needs early enough. I need to make my reasoning visible sooner, especially when other people have to build from it.

The design position I am claiming here is therefore not "I solve problems by calculating them." It is closer to this: I design best when I can make hidden assumptions explicit, compare options under evidence, and keep revising the representation of the problem when the first framing proves too narrow. The growth visible across these projects is a shift from pursuing the cleanest model to asking whether the model is serving the right stakeholders, the right stage of the process, and the right decision.

Design Identity

Strengths, risks, and current growth edges

Values

What I optimize for

  • Making assumptions and tradeoffs explicit
  • Using representations that change a decision
  • Linking analysis to stakeholder consequences
  • Giving shared work and teammates visible credit
Strengths

Where I am most reliable

  • Structuring ambiguous problems into testable components
  • Building analytical and computational decision supports
  • Comparing alternatives under explicit criteria
  • Translating technical evidence into concise explanations
Biases / Risks

What can distort my process

  • Converging too early once a numerical objective appears
  • Over-trusting clean models and under-representing build reality
  • Letting analytical tractability narrow the problem frame
  • Using representation as a substitute for direct testing if time is short
Growth Areas

What I need to do earlier

  • Represent constructability and communication before final convergence
  • Pair models with faster empirical checks or mockups
  • State what a simplification leaves out instead of hiding it
  • Keep design criteria broader than the easiest available metric

How This Position Appears In My Work

Project-by-project expression of the same design habits

Praxis I

Modeling to clarify a user-centered problem

The useful move was reframing loose straps as a coupled problem of comfort, access, adjustability, and snagging. My contribution pushed the team toward measurable proxies and concept comparison.

CIV102

Analysis was necessary but not sufficient

I was strongest when decomposing failure modes and building the analytical picture of the bridge. The project also exposed that representation must include constructability, not just capacity.

Gaussian

Abstraction as design choice

The project is the clearest example of my instinct to redesign the problem itself. The challenge was making that reduction useful without pretending it solved the general case.

Evolution

What changed across the three projects

Frame

Framing improved when the problem became more abstract

Praxis made me translate a visible nuisance into multiple user consequences. CIV102 showed that I can still frame too narrowly around efficiency. Gaussian is the strongest framing example because the core progress came from choosing the right reduction before doing more computation.

Diverge

Divergence is strongest when I force alternatives to stay visible

Praxis used concept branching most explicitly. The Gaussian project uses parameter sweeps and multiple channel regimes as a more analytical form of divergence. CIV102 diverged less than it should have because quantitative structure invited early narrowing.

Converge

Convergence is useful only when closure is earned

The bridge work taught that a strong-looking optimum is not enough if important constraints are still implicit. Gaussian convergence is more disciplined because exact-versus-bound comparison forces the simplification to justify itself.

Represent

Representation became a design tool, not just documentation

Praxis used a simulation interface to make prototype consequences visible. CIV102 drawings and calculations connected geometry to build decisions. Gaussian plots and heatmaps clarified where a bound remained useful and where it merely looked elegant.
