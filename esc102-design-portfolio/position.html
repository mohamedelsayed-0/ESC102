Position Statement

I design best when I can make the problem clear enough to test

As a student engineer, I do my best design work when I can turn ambiguity into something a team can inspect, test, and revise together. My main risk is trusting the first clean model too early, especially once a metric starts to feel more complete than the design situation really is.

Current context
First-year engineering work across team design, structural design, and individual analytical research
Strongest contribution
Making competing options legible enough to compare without pretending the problem is already solved
Known bias
Converging around the most tractable criterion before user or build reality is back in view

My Position On Design Practice

Formal Position Statement

This page states the design position that connects the project cases, the handbook entries, and the evidence pages. The goal is to make the pattern in my decisions clear before the project details begin.

I no longer think of engineering design as a neat path from technical rules to a single correct answer. What matters more to me is the interpretive work at the beginning: deciding what the real problem is, whose needs carry weight, which constraints are active, and what kind of evidence can actually help a team move. Design becomes valuable when it turns uncertainty into something people can reason about together.

Because of that, I now value divergence much more than I did at the start of the year. Diverging is not just a way to generate more ideas. It is a way to remove bias toward the first solution, or even toward the belief that there is no solution at all. At the beginning of Praxis II, I did not see the daycare wristband workflow as a proper viable opportunity and I even proposed to my team that there might be no solution worth pursuing. Once we diverged properly across parent-applied, staff-supported, and machine-assisted directions, the opportunity stopped looking empty and started looking structured. That experience changed how I think about the beginning of design work.

I still begin by trying to make the situation legible. In practice, that means identifying the stakeholders, naming the important failure cases, and choosing a representation that lets competing options be compared without pretending the problem is already solved. In Praxis I, that took the form of reframing loose straps as a broader issue involving comfort, access, adjustability, snagging, and manufacturability. In CIV102, it meant decomposing the bridge into interacting failure modes instead of treating the project as a single strength calculation. In the Gaussian project, it meant reducing the problem to a symmetric setting that could be interpreted at the parameter level rather than left as repeated numerical evaluation.

My instinct is to build a simplified model early, and that is both a strength and a weakness. It is a strength because a good model gives a team criteria, comparison, and a shared language for decision-making. It becomes a weakness when I start mistaking analytical neatness for design completeness, or when divergence stays open past the point where it is still helping the team distinguish between options. Praxis I taught me that divergence can run too long and compress convergence if there is no stop rule. Praxis II taught me the complementary lesson: divergence is worth protecting when it removes first-solution bias and reveals several viable paths before criteria narrow the set.

My position, then, is not that engineering design is best when it becomes fully calculable or that divergence is simply "good" because it is open-ended. I do my best design work when I can make assumptions explicit, diverge enough to remove bias and make the opportunity constructive, build representations that help compare options, and stay honest about what those representations leave out. The projects in this portfolio show both sides of that claim: the usefulness of structured reasoning and the need to keep reopening or redirecting the frame before a clean model narrows it too far.

Praxis I

Praxis II

The second blossom is stronger because it does not just generate more ideas. It turns an initially weak-looking opportunity into a constructive comparison between several workflow directions before convergence begins.

Position In Context

The values and tendencies a reader should know first

These points summarize the values, strengths, risks, and growth areas that shape how the rest of the portfolio is organized and interpreted.

Values

Clarity, accountable divergence, and team-readable reasoning

I care most about making a design situation legible enough that other people can inspect the reasoning, challenge the assumptions, and move the decision together. That now includes diverging deliberately enough to remove first-solution bias and make the opportunity look more constructive before convergence starts.

Strengths

I turn ambiguity into criteria and comparable options

My strongest move is building structure inside uncertainty: identifying what matters, choosing a workable representation, and turning vague preferences into an explicit comparison without pretending the first clean answer is already correct.

Biases / Risks

I can over-trust the cleanest model or dismiss an opportunity too early

Once a problem becomes tractable, I can start narrowing it too quickly. Earlier in a project, I can also mistake an underdeveloped opportunity for a bad one if divergence has not yet made the alternatives visible.

Growth Areas

I need clearer stop rules for divergence and earlier checks during convergence

The correction I am making is not less divergence. It is more deliberate divergence, followed by clearer criteria and earlier reality checks so the later comparison stays disciplined without becoming blind.

Divergence

Why I now diverge more deliberately

I used to think of divergence mostly as delay. I now see it as a bias-removal step that helps a team construct the opportunity properly before it converges. The goal is not endless branching. The goal is enough branching to challenge the first reaction, surface several real directions, and then narrow them with criteria.

What The Position Protects

The commitments I do not want speed or neatness to erase

Care

User and stakeholder consequences stay visible

I do not want efficiency or technical elegance to erase the lived effect of a design on the people who have to use, build, or interpret it.

Accountability

Reasoning should be inspectable

Models, drawings, and figures are most useful when they expose assumptions and make shared work easier to follow rather than hiding judgement behind authority.

Efficiency

Speed matters, but only with intention

I value moving quickly toward structure, but not if that speed narrows the problem around the easiest available metric before the important tradeoffs are in view.

Synthesis

What ties this statement together

I am claiming a design position built around interpretation, explicit criteria, and accountable simplification. Across the projects, the growth is not that I moved away from models. It is that I now trust them more when I also say what they leave out, when they should stop guiding the work, and what kind of evidence has to come back into view before I close the decision.
