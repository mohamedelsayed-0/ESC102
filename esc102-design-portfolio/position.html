Position Statement

I design best when I can make the problem clear enough to test

As a student engineer, I do my best design work when I can turn ambiguity into something a team can inspect, test, and revise together. My main risk is trusting the first clean model too early, especially once a metric starts to feel more complete than the design situation really is.

Current context
First-year engineering work across team design, structural design, and individual analytical research
Strongest contribution
Making competing options legible enough to compare without pretending the problem is already solved
Known bias
Converging around the most tractable criterion before user or build reality is back in view

My Position On Design Practice

Formal Position Statement

This is my current position in context as a student engineer. It is a revision of my earlier statement, not a copy of it. The connected project notes and CTMF entries on this page are there to test and support the claim, not replace it. It establishes the basis the rest of the portfolio will be read against.

I no longer think of engineering design as a neat path from technical rules to a single correct answer. What matters more to me is the interpretive work at the beginning: deciding what the real problem is, whose needs carry weight, which constraints are active, and what kind of evidence can actually help a team move. Design becomes valuable when it turns uncertainty into something people can reason about together.

Because of that, I usually begin by trying to make the situation legible. In practice, that means identifying the stakeholders, naming the important failure cases, and choosing a representation that lets competing options be compared without pretending the problem is already solved. In Praxis I, that took the form of reframing loose straps as a broader issue involving comfort, access, adjustability, snagging, and manufacturability. In CIV102, it meant decomposing the bridge into interacting failure modes instead of treating the project as a single strength calculation. In the Gaussian project, it meant reducing the problem to a symmetric setting that could be interpreted at the parameter level rather than left as repeated numerical evaluation.

My instinct is to build a simplified model early, and that is both a strength and a weakness. It is a strength because a good model gives a team criteria, comparison, and a shared language for decision-making. It becomes a weakness when I start mistaking analytical neatness for design completeness. Feedback on my earlier position statement helped make this clearer: saying that divergence "took a long time" was too vague. The more accurate point is that in Praxis, divergence ran past the point where it was still helping the team distinguish between options, which compressed convergence and left later decisions more rushed than they should have been. That is the design failure I am trying to correct, not divergence itself.

My position, then, is not that engineering design is best when it becomes fully calculable. It is that I do my best design work when I can make assumptions explicit, build representations that help compare options, and stay honest about what those representations leave out. The projects in this portfolio show both sides of that claim: the usefulness of structured reasoning and the need to keep reopening the frame before a clean model narrows it too far.

Position In Context

The values and tendencies a reader should know first

The IAT asks for more than a belief statement. It asks for the design values, abilities, strengths, and weaknesses that shape how the rest of the portfolio is organized and interpreted.

Values

Clarity, accountability, and team-readable reasoning

I care most about making a design situation legible enough that other people can inspect the reasoning, challenge the assumptions, and move the decision together.

Strengths

I turn ambiguity into criteria and comparable options

My strongest move is building structure inside uncertainty: identifying what matters, choosing a workable representation, and turning vague preferences into an explicit comparison.

Biases / Risks

I can over-trust the cleanest model or metric

Once a problem becomes tractable, I can start narrowing it too quickly. That risk shows up as premature convergence around what is easiest to calculate or display.

Growth Areas

I need to reopen the frame earlier and more deliberately

The correction I am making is to represent build reality, user consequences, and exclusions earlier, so the later comparison is disciplined without becoming blind.

Revision

How this statement changed from my earlier version

I did not carry the original position statement into this portfolio unchanged. The revision comes from what the projects made harder to deny about my practice.

Earlier emphasis

Structured reasoning was present, but the risk was underspecified

The earlier version recognized that I like structure, models, and decision support. What it did not state clearly enough was how that strength can turn into premature closure once a clean criterion appears.

What changed it

Three projects made the weakness concrete

Praxis exposed the cost of leaving divergence open too long and then converging too quickly. CIV102 showed that neat calculations are not yet design completeness. The Gaussian project made it impossible to ignore the obligation to state what an elegant reduction excludes.

Current statement

The position is now about legibility plus disciplined reopening

The current statement is more precise: I design best when I can make the problem clear enough to test, and I design badly when I let that clarity substitute for the full problem too early.

How I Work Under Uncertainty

The design loop I trust most

The sequence below is the pattern I return to when the problem is still unclear. The last step is the one I have learned not to skip: checking whether the model that helped me move quickly is now hiding something important.

1 Frame

Make the situation legible

Name stakeholders, constraints, and failure cases before deciding what success even looks like.

See it in

Praxis process note Evaluation criteria
2 Represent

Build a workable model

Use a sketch, simulation, drawing, or reduced mathematical form that makes alternatives comparable.

See it in

CIV102 process note Prototypes: graphical
3 Compare

Let evidence move the decision

Prefer criteria, calculations, plots, and tests that reveal what should change next, not just what looks polished.

See it in

Gaussian process note PIAA
4 Re-open

Check what the model excluded

Return to build reality, communication, and user consequences before treating the cleanest result as final.

See it in

CIV102 process note Verification

My main failure mode appears between steps three and four: once comparison becomes quantifiable, I can start closing the problem too quickly. The work in this portfolio is strongest when I resist that pull.

What The Position Protects

The commitments I do not want speed or neatness to erase

Care

User and stakeholder consequences stay visible

I do not want efficiency or technical elegance to erase the lived effect of a design on the people who have to use, build, or interpret it.

Accountability

Reasoning should be inspectable

Models, drawings, and figures are most useful when they expose assumptions and make shared work easier to follow rather than hiding judgement behind authority.

Efficiency

Speed matters, but only with intention

I value moving quickly toward structure, but not if that speed narrows the problem around the easiest available metric before the important tradeoffs are in view.

Synthesis

What ties this statement together

I am claiming a design position built around interpretation, explicit criteria, and accountable simplification. Across the projects, the growth is not that I moved away from models. It is that I now trust them more when I also say what they leave out, when they should stop guiding the work, and what kind of evidence has to come back into view before I close the decision.
