Position Statement

I design best when I can make the problem clear enough to test

As a student engineer, I do my best design work when I can turn an unclear situation into something a team can inspect, compare, and revise together. My main risk is still converging too quickly around the cleanest model once it starts to feel more complete than the design situation really is.

Design belief
Open the problem enough to remove bias before narrowing toward a defensible answer
Where I excel
Making unclear problems clear enough to test, compare, and explain with a team
Known bias
Trusting a neat criterion too early, especially before user or build reality is back in view

Position Statement // Overview of my beliefs as an Engineer

Engineering design, to me, is an iterative and interconnected way to remove bias, investigate possible solutions thoroughly, and reach the best outcome a team can actually justify.

This page states the design position I want the rest of the portfolio to be read against. The goal is not just to summarize projects, but to explain the commitments, habits, and corrections that now shape how I work.

What changed most this year is not that I stopped valuing structure. It is that I learned structure works best after the problem has been opened properly, not before.

Introduction

How my view of engineering design changed

Earlier in the year, I tended to treat design as something that improved once the right criterion appeared quickly. If the frame looked neat early, I was more willing to trust it and move toward comparison and convergence.

Engineering design now means something more specific to me: it is an iterative and interconnected tool for removing bias, investigating possible solutions thoroughly, and helping a team reach the best outcome it can actually justify.

Working through Praxis I, CIV102, Praxis II, and Gaussian changed that view. I still value criteria and models, but I now see that their usefulness depends on whether the team has first made the situation legible: who is affected, what tradeoffs are active, and what kind of evidence should actually count.

The clearest change for me came in Praxis II. At the beginning, I did not read the daycare wristband problem as a properly viable design opportunity and I even suggested there might be no solution worth pursuing. Once we diverged properly across several workflow directions, several workable options appeared. That experience changed how I think about the beginning of design.

What Engineering Design Means To Me

I now think of design as structuring uncertainty without hiding it too early

The process that feels most honest to me is to open the situation, remove first-solution bias, set filters, and then converge without forgetting what the filters leave out.

Design Philosophy

From unclear situation to testable direction

1

Read the situation

Map stakeholders, active constraints, and what is still unknown.

→
2

Diverge

Open several directions so the first reaction does not become the answer by default.

→
3

Set filters

Choose shared criteria that make the branches comparable and easier to judge.

→
4

Converge and test

Move toward the strongest option, then test what the model still leaves out.

Read the situation → diverge → set filters → converge and test

This is the sequence I now trust most. The work is not only to solve a problem, but to make the problem readable enough that convergence is deserved.

I no longer think of engineering design as a neat path from technical rules to one correct answer. What matters more to me is the interpretive work near the start: deciding what the real problem is, whose needs carry weight, which constraints are active, and what kind of evidence can actually help a team move.

Because of that, I now appreciate divergence much more than I did at the start of the year. I used to think of it mainly as delay. I now see it as a bias-removal step that can stop a team from converging around the first clean direction or, just as importantly, around the belief that there is no direction at all.

My best work still begins by making the situation legible. In Praxis I, that meant widening a loose-strap complaint into a broader carrying-system problem. In CIV102, it meant breaking the bridge into interacting failure modes instead of treating the project as one strength calculation. In Gaussian, it meant reducing the problem to a clearer analytical frame while staying honest about where that frame should stop.

What I am trying to protect is not neatness for its own sake. It is clarity that other people can inspect, challenge, and use. A model or criterion becomes valuable when it makes judgement easier to follow, not when it hides the judgement behind polish.

Divergence In Practice

What changed between Praxis I and Praxis II

These annotated reconstructions matter because they show how the same divergence tool behaved differently in two projects. Praxis I widened the opportunity, but it stayed open too long. Praxis II used the same tool more deliberately to remove bias, create a more constructive reading of the opportunity, and narrow with clearer filters.

Praxis I

Divergence helped, but it ran longer than the project could comfortably support

The blossom did useful work by preventing the team from jumping immediately to one fastening fix, but the branches stayed open past the point where they were still helping convergence. The result was a more compressed filtering step later.

Praxis II

Divergence removed bias and made the opportunity look workable

The second blossom is stronger because it does not just generate more ideas. It turns an initially weak-looking opportunity into a constructive comparison between several workflow directions before convergence begins.

Summary Of Position

What stayed the same, what changed, and what I am correcting now

This is the shortest version of the position I want a reader to carry into the rest of the portfolio.

Stayed the same

I still care most about human impact and readable reasoning

What has remained constant is the desire to make work that matters to people and to explain it clearly enough that others can inspect the logic behind it.

Changed

I now value divergence as part of serious design work

I no longer see divergence mainly as delay. I see it as a necessary way to remove bias, widen the frame, and give a team a more constructive understanding of the opportunity before narrowing it.

Now

I am trying to pair clearer stop rules with earlier reality checks

The correction I am making is not less structure. It is better timing: open the problem properly, set filters earlier, and keep checking when a clean model is outrunning the actual situation.

Values, Strengths, Risks, Growth

I value clarity, accountable divergence, and team-readable reasoning. My strongest move is turning ambiguity into criteria and comparable options. My risk is over-trusting the cleanest model or dismissing an opportunity too early. My growth area is to use clearer stop rules and earlier reality checks once convergence begins.

At the beginning of Praxis II, I did not think the wristband problem was a properly viable design opportunity and I even proposed that there might be no solution worth pursuing. The divergence work corrected that judgement. Once the team opened the problem properly, several viable directions appeared and the opportunity became something we could act on constructively.

Project Evidence

The projects that make this position concrete

These cases show the position in use: open the situation, compare real alternatives, and keep the limits of each model or criterion visible.

Praxis I

I learned that divergence can widen the frame and still overrun its timing

Praxis I first showed me that the problem itself needed interpretation. It also showed me that useful divergence can still last too long if the team does not yet have a clear stop rule or filter structure.

CIV102

I learned that strong analysis still has to answer to build reality

CIV102 made the value of structured comparison even clearer, but it also showed that a clean analytical answer is only convincing if it remains accountable to constructability, sequencing, and the physical logic of the bridge as a system.

Praxis II

I learned that divergence can reveal opportunity where I first saw none

Praxis II changed my position most directly. I began by thinking the opportunity might not be viable. Divergence corrected that reaction by opening several workflow paths, which made the problem readable enough for the team to converge on a workable direction.

Gaussian

I learned to say more clearly what an elegant model leaves out

The Gaussian project sharpened the other side of my position. Simplification can create real analytical leverage, but it also creates responsibility. A clean argument is strongest when it includes the limits of the frame that made it possible.

Across these projects, the common pattern is that I work best when I can make assumptions explicit, compare real alternatives, and stay honest about when a model or criterion has started narrowing the work too far.
